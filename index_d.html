Exploiting low-rank structures in audio signal restoration problems

Applications of non-negative matrix factorisation (NMF) techniques in audio signal processing typically adopt an analysis approach—factorisation is performed on the squared magnitude of the analysis coefficients returned by a certain time-frequency transform. Conversely, the use of NMF techniques in synthesis}-based models, where the amplitude of a signal over time is characterised explicitly, has been somewhat limited. A synthesis approach is desirable when the data degradation mechanism operates directly in the temporal domain (e.g., quantisation, clipping, missing data), since its effects on the spectral domain might defy an easy characterisation. With the aim of exploiting low-rank structures in synthesis-based models at a feasible computational expense, this article builds upon the so-called low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model and proposes an efficient expectation-maximisation (EM) algorithm to estimate the latent low-rank structure of the variance of the synthesis coefficients. Marginalisation of the coefficients and nuisance parameters such as the background noise level σ2 is carried out numerically by means of a lightweight MCMC sampler, thus circumventing the need of prohibitive matrix inversion operations. The proposed MCMC sampler exploits the structure of the chosen dictionary—the complex modified discrete cosine transform (CMDCT)—to perform efficient joint draws. Moreover, the non-negative factorisation sub-problem is supplemented with domain-specific regularisation schemes aimed at fostering high-quality audio reconstructions. The proposed strategy is tested on a battery of denoising tasks and its potential for signal decomposition is explored in a series of case studies.

Case study 1: comparison with min-vol KL-NMF

For comparison purposes, the KL-NMF method with minimum volume pensalisation [Leplat et al., 2020], [Leplat et al., 2021] is run on the clean signal. The extracted components are arranged such that they match as closely as possible the sources identified with the proposed LRTFS-EM method.

Although KL-NMF with minimum volume pensalisation manages to decompose the signal in meaningful components, the distinction between sources is not as sharp as that obtained with the proposed strategy. While the first component (KL-NMF with min-vol) is virtually identical to that of LRTFS-EM (except for continuity/smoothness issues), the second component (KL-NMF with min-vol) merges two different notes in one source. These notes correspond to sources 2 and 3 of the LRTFS-EM decomposition.

Case study 1: comparison with alternate minimisation LRTFS (piano excerpt with input SNR = 10 dB)

The optimisation strategy proposed in [Fevotte and Kowalski, 2018] addresses a model similar to that adopted in this work, but relies on an alternate minimisation strategy between the low-rank matrices (W, H) and the synthesis coefficients and an annealing scheme (i.e., controlled gradual decrease) of the unknown background noise level σ2. Conversely, LRTFS-EM targets (W, H) directly by marginalising both the synthesis coefficients and the noise level σ2—hence the solution is less sensitive to initial estimation of these parameters. Moreover, the temporal smoothness penalisation on activation coefficients H seems to improve the continuity of the identified sources and the overall solution. In the table below, the result reported in [Fevotte and Kowalski, 2018], where their alternate-minimisation scheme is applied to the same piano excerpt but with background noise set at SNR = 20 dB, is compared with the LRTFS-EM solution for the same input.

Case study 3: Early jazz recording with piano and trumpet (input SNR = 15 dB)

The LRTFS-EM method is applied to a 25 second long early jazz recording contaminated with additive white Gaussian background noise. The low-rank components can be loosely separated between the piano parts (black) and the trumpet melody (red). Although the decomposition is not perfect—the components identified as piano parts still retain a small contribution of the trumpet melody at the beginning and the end of the signal—, the trumpet components stay mostly silent during the piano solo in the middle. Moreover, the denoised signal (LRTFS-EM solution) exhibits high perceptual quality and contains limited presence of artifacts.

The LRTFS-EM solution is compared with denoising methods based on strucutred sparsity. In particular, the reconstruction obtained with the two-layer representation proposed in [Fevotte et al., 2007] for tonal and transient separation is included alongside our own sparse solution (we adopted the two-layer representation of [Fevotte et al., 2007] but used the Complex Modified Discrete Cosine Transform rather than the MDCT dictionary suggested in that work. Coefficients are computed using an MCMC simulation similar to that [Wolfe et al., 2004]. Although the LRTFS-EM solution contains less artifacts than the MCMC reconstructions and performs similarly in terms of output SNR, it fails to recover the "brightness" of the clean signal. The vocal parts, in particular, might sound somewhat opaque and muffled. We conjecture that the use of NMF-based decomposition in speech and singing voice analysis might require a larger number of low-rank components (e.g., [Bilen et al., 2015] reports K = 10 for music and K = 28 for human voice) and the incorporation of alternative modelling assumptions—the temporal smoothness assumptions adopted in [Wolfe et al., 2004], [Fevotte et at., 2007] and this work might be incomplete or inaccurate in certain settings.
